*Thread Reply:* Ditto - a perfect 100 often tells as much about the data and it does about the model. From a production perspective, this means ‚Äòwith real data, expect good performance, but not THAT good'. A good look at the data should tell more. A major factor contributing to data being 'easy' is the camera perspective being homogenous concerning the animal‚Äôs features. This can be due to pictures taken in burst mode, or an alignment pre-processing step. Creating images that look very similar or ‚Äònear duplicates‚Äô.

Sometimes you might get these ‚Äònear-duplicates‚Äô in clusters. Think of 3x2 bursts of 2 images of the same individual captured during 3 separate encounters with some time between encounters. Each of the same encounter pairs will be much more easily matchable against its counterpart than it will be with the rest. In such cases, top-1 accuracy is misleading as the perfect score can be achieved by correctly matching just one of all valid samples. mAP is more balanced for capturing performance across all valid samples - some of which can be more diverse. Drawback is that mAP is less directly interpretable. High rank-1, low mAP is often a sign of ‚Äônear-duplicates‚Äô being present in the data.

Thanks for sharing the work @Anton Alvarez. Also, cool to see MegaDescriptor zero-shotting this.

*Thread Reply:* @Anton Alvarez I agree. Hotspotter's visualizations are easy to understand and relate to. MiewID's Grad-CAM based visualizations are good for checking in on training results, but they're not very useful in a production environment when evaluating match candidates. Worse, deep learning can pick up on features that are less relatable to humans than more obvious patterning (e.g., beluga dorsal ridges versus scars), and adding visualization can significantly add to inference time and eat up disk space. Wild Me might run 2-5k ID jobs per day across our platforms on average, and we actually turn off MiewID visualization in most cases to optimize for speed. Even for Hotspotter, we try to delete visualizations within two weeks as they eat up disk space quickly. Visualization is very much an active field of research, and we're looking for solutions that are better than Grad-CAM and more relatable.

*Thread Reply:* @Jess Tam Hi Jess, example notebooks are great to get going fast. They are done on a Beluga dataset that is single-viewpoint, though. For multiple distinct viewpoints (such as left vs right) for species that do not exhibit bilateral symmetry, the go-to approach is to handle each viewpoint+name combination as a separate individual. This can be done by configuring the name_keys argument. Conversely, there are cases where this is too aggressive, such as when you have viewpoints like 'frontleft', 'frontright' and are still partially matchable. In such cases you might opt to let the model learn to match cross-viewpoint, or map them to common 'left' and 'right' depending on experimentation. The model gets quite good at partial matching if the dataset allows for it.

Our general pipeline also includes orientation normalization - rotation only. This is done for legacy for compatibility with other algos we use. The CNN backbone is good at learning invariance. We have been trying to reduce that requirement by training with rotation augmentation, so that the model is not handicapped by it - so not a hard requirement. It is still not fully invariant to extreme (30deg+) rotation for most of the terrestrial species.
