Hi @Keenan Johnson, I was waiting for someone more qualified to answer this but I can give some info.

Camera traps made by Reconyx, Browning, Bushnell are still the heavy hitters among many wildlife biologists, at least the ones I interact with. They've got their drawbacks, but they're generally quite reliable, with Reconyx generally being the "highest end" camera trap.

Unfortunately, there are no widely adopted open source options at the moment. Most commercially available camera traps rely on ASIC hardware and everything is quite proprietary. Many have tried to remedy this with custom open source solutions (I have a long term project related to this). You'll find a plethora of Raspi based solutions, but they won't have the battery life or trigger time needed for most studies. Andrew Quitmeyer's MothBox is probably the best version of this I have seen, though it's primarily targeted at insect studies (and does a good job at that!).

I've also seen some solutions proposed based on the OpenMV camera system, which seem a bit more promising, but still untested. There are some other small production camera traps that exist, but I know less about them. I recently learned about these smart cameras, which use a cool low-power LiDAR instead of classical PIR sensing https://fielddata.tech/.

CXL's Sentinel is now being field tested, and Microsoft announced their Sparrow project, both of which are super impressive. But I dont think they're open source or purchasable at the moment.

If you let me know what you're trying to work on, I might be able to offer some more specific advice!
