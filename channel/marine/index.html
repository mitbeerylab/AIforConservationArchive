As per the previous thread on this channel, I think we're getting close as a community to having a sufficiently diverse collection of public datasets where fish have been annotated with bounding boxes (or segmentation labels that could be used as bounding boxes) that one could train a pretty general-purpose fish detector that would facilitate data review and downstream species classifier training. I'm thinking of BRUV-like deployments where the visual concept of "fish" matches the colloquial concept of the word, i.e. not things like fish in drone images seen from above the surface, or microscopic images, or fish on the decks of ships, etc. Basically fish that look like 🐟, 🐠, 🦈, etc. In a perfect world I'd like to do other high-level taxa that would appear in the same videos (e.g. crabs), but it doesn't look like the state of public data is quite there for other taxa yet.

So... to move the ball forward a little, I'm inclined to at least set the table for getting metadata for all of the public datasets that fit the bill into a common format (preferably COCO). After a thread on the general channel here, a few of us just did this for aerial and drone wildlife datasets:

https://github.com/agentmorris/agentmorrispublic/blob/main/drone-datasets.md

...and I'd like to do basically exactly the same thing for "boxes on fish" datasets. IMO if you can download the data, load the annotations, and render random annotations onto sample images, you're 99% of the way to converting to a standard format.

Questions for this channel:

  1. Is anyone already working on a general fish detector that kind of fits this vibe?

  2. Is anyone else already working on aggregating all of those datasets into a common format?

  3. Am I missing any boxes-on-fish (where segmentation masks can be reduced to boxes) at https://lila.science/otherdatasets#images-marine?

  4. Is anyone else sufficiently fired up to lead this exercise? 🙂

Accelerating Species Recognition and Labelling of Fish From Underwater Video With Machine-Assisted Deep Learning
Machine-assisted object detection and classification of fish species from Baited Remote Underwater Video Station (BRUVS) surveys using deep learning algorithms presents an opportunity for optimising analysis time and rapid reporting of marine ecosystem statuses. Training object detection algorithms for BRUVS analysis presents significant challenges: the model requires training datasets with bounding boxes already applied identifying the location of all fish individuals in a scene, and it requires training datasets identifying species with labels. In both cases, substantial volumes of data are required and this is currently a manual, labour-intensive process, resulting in a paucity of the labelled data currently required for training object detection models for species detection. Finding Nemo&amp;rsquo;s Giant Cousin: Keypoint Matching for Robust Re-Identification of Giant Sunfish
The Giant Sunfish (Mola alexandrini) has unique patterns on its body, which allow for individual identification. By continuously gathering and matching images, it is possible to monitor and track individuals across location and time. However, matching images manually is a tedious and time-consuming task. To automate the process, we propose a pipeline based on finding and matching keypoints between image pairs. We evaluate our pipeline with four different keypoint descriptors, namely ORB, SIFT, RootSIFT, and SuperPoint, and demonstrate that the number of matching keypoints between a pair of images is a strong indicator for the likelihood that they contain the same individual. The best results are obtained with RootSIFT, which achieves an mAP of 75.91% on our test dataset (TinyMola+) without training or fine-tuning any parts of the pipeline. Furthermore, we show that the pipeline generalizes to other domains, such as re-identification of seals and cows. Lastly, we discuss the impracticality of a ranking-based output for real-life tasks and propose an alternative approach by viewing re-identification as a binary classification. I agree with @Malte Pedersen that implementing a simple tracker yourself could actually give you better results and be easier to understand. A lot of papers in MOT are overly complicated because they are competing over a couple percentage points on human tracking benchmarks.

BotSort is based on a Kalman filter approach but has a few extra things bolted on that make it hard to debug. In general I think the trackers included in YOLOv8 are hard to debug and you would probably have an easier time implementing a separate tracker yourself. The original version of SORT could be a good starting point, you will have control over the hyperparameters and can see how changing certain parameters affects performance: https://github.com/abewley/sort

Regarding the boxes post-tracking being different from the detected boxes, that is a property of Kalman filtering based approaches. Basically, the Kalman filter is doing two things in each frame: (1) predicting where existing tracks will move to based on their velocity, (2) associating new detections with existing tracks based on how close the new detections are to the predicted locations of existing tracks from step 1. The final output from the tracker is kind of like an average of the two, because a Kalman filter was designed for systems that have both noisy measurements (= detections in this case) and a noisy motion model -- so they assume the "real" position is somewhere between the two.

Species distribution models (SDMs) address the whereabouts of species and are central to ecology. Deep learning (DL) is poised to further elevate the already significant role of SDMs in ecology and conservation, but the potential and limitations of this transformation are still largely unassessed.<br><br>We evaluate DL SDMs for 2,299 terrestrial vertebrate and invertebrate species at continental scale and 1km resolution in a like-for-like comparison with latest implementation of classic SDMs. We compare two DL methods (a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) on point covariates and a convolutional neural network (CNN) on geospatial patches) against existing SDMs (Maxent and Random Forest). Yup that's it basically. It goes something like this: (i) embed the whole dataset; (ii) embed a single target sample (a target fish sound in our case); (iii) cosine similarity search the embedded dataset to find closest matches to this embedded sample; (iv) label closest matches as positive or negative; (v) train a linear classifier on labelled data; (vi) classify entire embedded dataset with said classifier; (vii) pick a logit threshold to label some more samples around, usually close to 0 where the model is most uncertain; (viii) repeat v through viii.

This might be quite similar to other active learning approaches, though I'm no expert in that. The 'Search for Squawk' paper I shared in the post above builds off this 2023 paper that coined the agile modelling term: https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2302.12948

Along the way I learnt a whole bunch of tricks that helped during the process and found it to be as much of an art as science in what logit threshold to pick, whether to focus on labelling more positive or negatives, what to do when even you as the human labeler aren't sure, etc. If anyone else reads this and wants to know more then I'd be happy to share.

While crowdsourcing has served the vision community well for most objective tasks (such as labeling a "zebra"), it now falters on tasks where there is substantial subjectivity in the concept (such as identifying "gourmet tuna"). However, empowering any user to develop a classifier for their concept is technically difficult: users are neither machine learning experts, nor have the patience to label thousands of examples. We compare this user driven process with the traditional crowdsourcing paradigm and find that the crowd's notion often differs from that of the user's, especially as the concepts become more subjective.
