<p>Hi everyone, we're excited to introduce The Caltech Fish Counting Dataset (CFC): A Benchmark for Multiple-Object Tracking and Counting, to appear at ECCV 2022!</p> <p>CFC is a large-scale dataset for detecting, tracking, and counting fish in sonar videos, an important conservation application and a rich data source for advancing low signal-to-noise computer vision applications and tackling domain generalization in tracking and counting.</p> <p>Sonar video is used to monitor salmon during their seasonal migration, informing sustainable fisheries management. Manually counting fish in these videos requires significant effort. Automation using computer vision could reduce this burden and help scale up monitoring efforts.</p> <p>To enable this, we collected over half a million annotations in over 1,500 videos sourced from seven different sonar cameras, allowing researchers to train multiple-object tracking and counting algorithms and evaluate generalization performance at unseen test locations.</p> <p>We encourage others to check out the dataset (linked below) and join us in tackling these challenges! The Giant Sunfish (Mola alexandrini) has unique patterns on its body, which allow for individual identification. *Thread Reply:* Seeing some video might also help with diagnosing what's going on. Can I ask what you need tracking for? (You mentioned you don't care about identity splits -- so wondering what it is you want out of a tracker). I agree with @Malte Pedersen that implementing a simple tracker yourself could actually give you better results and be easier to understand. A lot of papers in MOT are overly complicated because they are competing over a couple percentage points on human tracking benchmarks.

BotSort is based on a Kalman filter approach but has a few extra things bolted on that make it hard to debug. In general I think the trackers included in YOLOv8 are hard to debug and you would probably have an easier time implementing a separate tracker yourself. The original version of SORT could be a good starting point, you will have control over the hyperparameters and can see how changing certain parameters affects performance: https://github.com/abewley/sort

Regarding the boxes post-tracking being different from the detected boxes, that is a property of Kalman filtering based approaches. Basically, the Kalman filter is doing two things in each frame: (1) predicting where existing tracks will move to based on their velocity, (2) associating new detections with existing tracks based on how close the new detections are to the predicted locations of existing tracks from step 1. The final output from the tracker is kind of like an average of the two, because a Kalman filter was designed for systems that have both noisy measurements (= detections in this case) and a noisy motion model -- so they assume the "real" position is somewhere between the two.
